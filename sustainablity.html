Why Sustainability Matters to Us 🌿

At Corozal Bay EcoAdventures, sustainability is not a marketing term — it is the foundation of everything we do. CBEA was created specifically to reduce SACD's reliance on grants by generating revenue through responsible ecotourism, while ensuring that every visit protects ecosystems, uplifts communities, and creates lasting change. Belize is recognised by the UNWTO as one of the world's top four eco/sustainable tourism destinations, and CBEA is proud to be at the heart of that recognition.

⛳ The Triple Bottom Line

CBEA measures its success through the Triple Bottom Line — three equally important pillars that guide every decision, every experience, and every partnership we form.

🌿

Ecological

Protecting mangroves, coral reefs, seagrass beds, and iconic wildlife like the West Indian manatee through responsible tourism protocols.

🤝

Social

52 families across Sarteneja, Chunox, and Corozal benefit directly — women-led homestays, lionfish artisans, PescaTours families, and local guides.

🍀

Economic

Generating sustainable revenue to reduce SACD's grant dependency and creating stable employment — diversifying livelihoods beyond traditional fishing.

✅ Our Best Practices

🌿 Environmental Practices

  • ✔ Wastewater treatment systems in place at CBEA facilities
  • ✔ Solar panel installation currently underway at Warrie Bight
  • ✔ Mandatory life jackets & responsible no-fins snorkelling protocol to protect coral
  • ✔ Sun-protection clothing encouraged — no chemical sunscreen in reef areas
  • ✔ Tourism carrying capacity studies planned for snorkelling and archaeological zones
  • ✔ Active work to eliminate single-use plastics across all supplier operations

🤝 Social Practices

  • ✔ Inclusion of diverse community families across all tourism activities
  • ✔ Women empowered through cooking, homestays, lionfish crafts, and nature guiding
  • ✔ Transparent social responsibility policies against child labour and exploitation
  • ✔ Training programmes for local guides and community members each season
  • ✔ Annual financial reports shared openly with the community and SACD

🍀 Economic Practices

  • ✔ 80% of all tourism revenues flow directly into the local community value chain
  • ✔ Payments to guides, cooks, artisans, and fishing families set by the community itself
  • ✔ 20% supports SACD conservation operations and future reinvestment
  • ✔ Local suppliers and producers prioritised for all purchases
  • ✔ Financial transparency through annual reports to community and all stakeholders

📊 SWOT Analysis

An honest assessment of where CBEA stands today — and where we are heading.

▲ Strengths

  • Business model with strong social and environmental purpose
  • SACD co-manages protected areas — manatee and reef habitats
  • Strong relationships with local partner organisations
  • Network of collaborators and experts across many fields
  • Facilities designed for multiple tourism activities

▼ Weaknesses

  • Financial sustainability still depends on the NGO
  • Operational processes not yet fully defined
  • Client base still under active development
  • Dependence on a single accommodation establishment
  • Limited promotional and sales personnel

↗ Opportunities

  • Proximity to international airports — Belize, Chetumal, Tulum
  • Proximity to Mexican border and Maya World tourism route
  • Growing global demand for eco and sustainable tourism
  • Increase in outdoor activity bookings post-pandemic
  • Community preserves authentic Maya and fishing heritage

⚠ Threats

  • High vulnerability to weather events and public health crises
  • Similar ecotourism offerings growing in the Belize market
  • Risk of coastal development and mangrove deforestation
  • Ecosystem pollution and illegal fishing pressures
  • Potential changes in tourism and environmental legislation
